# Foundations

## What a serious display must do

A useful visual display joins numbers, words, and marks into one argument. It gives readers access to the overall pattern, inspectable local evidence, and the context required to challenge the claim. Simplicity is earned by removing obstacles to evidence, not by removing evidence itself.

## The embedded method

1. **Preserve the important dimensions.** Keep time, location, denominator, population, uncertainty, and relevant alternatives visible. When aggregation, projection, or filtering loses a dimension, state what is lost and provide an audit path when material.
2. **Join overview, detail, and context.** A reader should see the pattern, inspect individual values, and find source/definition/qualification without remembering a different view.
3. **Separate layers by visual weight.** Primary marks answer the question. Context permits comparison. Guides and containers are quieter. Explanatory text belongs next to the evidence.
4. **Repeat constant frames.** When the same question applies to several groups or periods, use aligned small multiples or panels with constant scales and ordering. Let the data vary, not the design.
5. **Spend colour as evidence.** A hue must encode measure, meaningful grouping, sequence, or question-specific focus. A named category does not automatically deserve colour. Preserve a usable grayscale reading.
6. **Tell narratives without destroying evidence.** An event, route, timeline, or sequence must retain its order and local detail. A persuasive title cannot replace the underlying temporal or spatial relation.
7. **Match visual effect to data effect.** Position, length, area, width, angle, and colour intensity can all create a perceived magnitude. Use the weakest sufficient encoding and test whether scale, perspective, or a changing design exaggerates the value.
8. **Treat graphics as a three-competence task.** Defend domain meaning, statistical validity, and visual construction independently. A beautiful chart cannot repair a weak measure or unsupported inference.
9. **Use data-built structure.** Reference lines, intervals, direct values, ordering, and repeated cycles may earn their ink when they make evidence easier to read. Decorative grids, pseudo-3D, texture, hatching, and framing do not.
10. **Choose the reading surface deliberately.** A sentence, table, table-graphic, chart, small-multiple set, or narrative display can be correct. Choose the one that best supports the reader's task, not the most fashionable mark.

## Operational consequences

- A bar or area starts at zero because length or area is read from a baseline.
- A line or point scale may be restricted only when zero is not the relevant comparator, the domain is explicit, and the restriction does not fabricate a story.
- Direct labels often remove the need for a legend or categorical palette.
- A dense table-graphic can be more analytical than a sparse chart when exact lookup matters.
- A dual scale is a burden of proof, not a convenience; use aligned panels or indexing when it would create a false correspondence.
- A rule is a diagnostic, not a substitute for looking. Document a justified exception, the rejected alternative, and the reader benefit.
