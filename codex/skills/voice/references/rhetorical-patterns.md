# Rhetorical Patterns

## Explanatory and institutional prose

**Pattern:** decision -> actor -> reason -> timing -> affected person -> next step.

Use a direct first sentence. Define acronyms before repeating them. Translate rules into eligibility, action, cost, option, consequence, and owner. Retain material scope: a partial restriction must remain partial.

## Argument

**Pattern:** sharp proposition -> fair evidence -> hinge -> qualification -> earned conclusion.

Use contrast as thought. `But` must turn the argument; `and` may hold two truths together; `yet` can preserve tension. Do not use polemical heat to skip the evidence.

## Narrative nonfiction

**Pattern:** arresting observed moment -> prompt orientation -> selected scene -> causal/chronological movement -> widened consequence or restrained close.

Start in action only if you can soon locate the reader. Choose details that let the reader infer emotion or significance. A small physical fact can do more than an adjective telling the reader to be moved.

Do not recap the fact packet after telling it. Do not write `the facts were simple`, `the full sequence`, `the outcome was`, or a similar explanation of the prose the reader has just read. Let the sequence, selected detail, and final supported consequence do the work.

## Paired news and narrative exercise

With the same fact packet, the two forms have different jobs:

| Form | First duty | Order | Common failure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| News brief | deliver indispensable facts fast | event -> people -> response -> outcome | duplicate recap added to meet a word target |
| Narrative follow-up | let a reader experience a supported sequence | consequential moment -> orientation -> selective movement -> resolved consequence | a report rewritten as choppy fact-by-fact restatement |

Meet a word target by selecting material detail or shaping transitions, never by restating a fact. If the source cannot support a richer scene, keep the narrative concise or state the constraint rather than manufacture drama. The same rule applies to a news brief: do not duplicate its outcome merely to reach a minimum word count.

### Source-sufficiency check

A fact packet supports narrative only when it supplies an observed moment plus enough of action, setting, sequence, person, object, dialogue, perception, or consequential change to form a scene. Bare incident metadata - time, distance, count, response, outcome - supports a news brief, not a 120-word narrative.

When the source is too thin, say so plainly: give the direct brief if useful, identify the scene material needed, and do not pad with recap or invention.

### Image discipline

An image must be either a verified observation or a clearly non-factual structural choice. Do not convert a likely consequence into an asserted fact: water near boxes does not establish soaked cardboard; an emergency does not establish fear or urgency; a quiet room does not establish silence. Use supplied objects, movements, sequence, and change. If richer sensory writing is requested, ask for observed scene material.

## Profile or scene

**Pattern:** exact notice -> concrete analogy or action -> human voice -> short landing.

Move between the observed detail and the larger human meaning. Address the reader only to make perception shared, not to manufacture intimacy.

## Speech and persuasion

**Pattern:** plain claim -> rising particulars -> controlled repetition -> concise imperative or conclusion.

Use parallelism and anaphora when the audience needs to hear sequence, accumulation, or contrast. Repetition must build pressure or clarity; it cannot replace an argument.

## Sentence architectures

| Form | Use when | Watch for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Loose/cumulative | action unfolds naturally after an early main clause | a tail that trails without purpose |
| Periodic | suspense or end-weight is worth delaying the main point | a prefatory maze that loses the reader |
| Balanced | contrast, comparison, calm judgment, memorable emphasis | artificial symmetry or a false equivalence |
| Parallel list | multiple causes, steps, harms, or promises need to be scanned | items with different grammatical jobs |
| One-sentence paragraph | a turn, verdict, release, or impact deserves isolation | melodramatic overuse |

## Deliberate exceptions

- Retain tact where direct naming would be cruel, unsafe, legally premature, or socially clumsy.
- Retain ambiguity in art, diplomacy, or emotion when the reader needs to experience competing meanings.
- Retain a technical term when it carries a distinction ordinary language loses; define it.
- Retain a long sentence when its rhythm and navigable structure make it clearer than fragments.
- Retain repetition when it establishes reference, progression, humour, or cadence.
