# High-Stakes Writing

Apply this protocol to policy, benefits, insurance, contracts, finance, safety, health, technical incidents, and public instructions. In these forms, opacity can change what a reader does, receives, loses, or believes.

## Translate the rule into a reader decision

Answer, in this order where relevant:

1. Who is affected or eligible?
2. What happens, is required, covered, excluded, promised, or prohibited?
3. Who owns the action or decision?
4. What evidence, condition, threshold, or definition governs it?
5. When does it begin, end, expire, or need action?
6. What does it cost, risk, change, or prevent?
7. What option, remedy, appeal, or next step remains?

Do not answer a question the source does not settle. Mark an unresolved material point rather than inventing a friendly answer.

## Meaning-under-pressure audit

For every loaded term, ask whether the draft has silently changed:

- **intent:** false, mistaken, negligent, deliberate, fraudulent;
- **causation:** followed, contributed, caused, enabled, prevented;
- **certainty:** may, likely, will, must, proved;
- **scope:** some access, all access, temporary restriction, closure;
- **responsibility:** team, named role, contractor, regulator, anonymous process;
- **coverage:** restore, treat, maintain, improve, exclude;
- **financial effect:** fee, cost, saving, loss, risk, obligation.

Use the ordinary term unless a technical/legal term carries a necessary distinction. Define that term before using it as a conclusion.

## Incident and accountability pattern

Write: **event -> known information -> responsible roles -> missed/actual action -> outcome -> corrective action -> remaining uncertainty**.

Avoid process language that replaces action: `steps were taken`, `a review was conducted`, `the matter was progressed`, `the system did not function as intended`. Name the supported role and act. Do not overstate individual blame when the evidence establishes only a system failure.

## Policy and benefits pattern

Write the reader version beside or before the formal rule:

- direct summary;
- eligibility/condition;
- action and deadline;
- inclusion and exclusion;
- appeal, contact, or next decision.

Preserve the formal rule's scope. Plain language explains the rule; it does not amend it.

## Financial and marketing claims

Translate every attractive label into mechanism: who pays, who bears risk, what is measured, what could change, what is excluded, and when. Treat unexplained jargon, euphemism, branding, and vague superlatives as a red flag requiring evidence.
