# Framework

## The six obligations

Use all six. A paragraph can be brief and still fail any one of them.

| Obligation | Reader must be able to recover | Typical failure | First repair |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Truth | fact, scope, certainty, condition, and term | stronger claim than evidence supports | restore the exact qualifier or operational definition |
| Agency | who knows, acts, decides, pays, or suffers | passive evasion or faceless process | name the actor and action |
| Order | point before the reader needs its evidence | prefatory thicket, late definition, buried conclusion | build a spine and resequence |
| Concreteness | person, thing, act, place, number, scene, or consequence | generality masquerading as thought | supply the telling particular |
| Economy | why each word is needed | nominalisation, duplicated sense, padded connector | delete, condense, or convert to a verb |
| Life | a human voice, momentum, and a landing | metronome sentences, decorative intensity, dead ending | vary form; choose a stronger final movement |

## Reader-first order

Information must arrive at the moment the reader needs it. Use this default progression:

1. **Orientation:** where are we, or what is at stake?
2. **Point:** what happened, is true, or must happen?
3. **Mechanism:** how, why, or on what evidence?
4. **Qualification:** what limits, exception, uncertainty, or counterargument matters?
5. **Consequence:** what changes for the reader, person, institution, or argument?
6. **Landing:** end on the clearest fact, image, choice, or implication - not a recycled abstraction.

Omit stages that do no work. Reverse the first two only for purposeful narrative suspense; settle the question promptly.

## The abstraction ladder

Move up to state an idea and down to prove it. Do not leave the reader stranded at either level.

| Level | Proper job | Example form | Danger |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Principle | frame an argument | fairness, safety, accountability | slogan without evidence |
| System | explain an operation | a claims rule, a hiring process | machinery without human stakes |
| Event | show what happened | a manager rejected the claim | isolated anecdote |
| Particular | make the fact felt or measurable | date, object, number, spoken choice | decorative clutter |

An effective paragraph often rises from a particular to a principle or falls from a principle to a particular. The movement is the reasoning.

## The sentence as a path

The ordinary line is **actor -> active verb -> object -> consequence**. It is a default, not a law. Preserve passive voice when the doer is unknown, the receiver is the news, tact or law warrants reticence, or an active subject would delay the verb beyond comprehension.

Make long sentences carry a visible series of thought-groups. Use punctuation to mark their stages. A short sentence is for a turn, a shock, a close, or a release - not a substitute for connections.

## Meaning before synonym

Words are not interchangeable labels. Before replacing a word, test:

- ordinary meaning and operational/legal meaning;
- degree, time, causation, and certainty;
- implication of intention or moral responsibility;
- register: plain, technical, comic, formal, intimate, or evasive;
- whether the new word alters what a reader can do or expect.

Prefer an exact ordinary word to a grand near-synonym. Define a necessary technical word before relying on it.

## Clarity and force coexist

Clear prose may be lyrical, witty, tactful, indirect, ambiguous, angry, or tender when the purpose requires it. The test is whether the reader experiences the intended effect without losing the factual and logical path.
