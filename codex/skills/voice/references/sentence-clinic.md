# Sentence Clinic

Use this clinic before shortening a troublesome sentence. Diagnose the load; do not merely cut words.

## The seven questions

1. What is the sentence's main assertion?
2. Who is the main actor, and what does that actor do?
3. What object, person, condition, or consequence receives the action?
4. What must remain qualified, conditional, attributed, or uncertain?
5. Which logical relation connects the parts: cause, contrast, time, condition, concession, or result?
6. What must a reader hold in mind before reaching the main verb?
7. Can a reader restate the sentence accurately after one attentive reading?

## Common diagnoses and repairs

| Diagnosis | Symptom | Repair |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Predatory opening | 20+ words of `if`, `given`, `following`, `in light of`, or background before the point | begin with the decision/event; move the condition after it |
| Buried verb | the sentence's action appears after a chain of nouns and prepositions | restore a subject plus finite verb near the start |
| Split family | subject, verb, object, or modifier are separated by an intrusive clause | reunite the grammatical family; relocate the interruption |
| False coordination | `and`, `but`, or `therefore` connects ideas without a real relation | split, replace with the true connector, or change the order |
| Negative maze | multiple negatives or qualified denials conceal the proposition | state the positive fact, keeping real uncertainty |
| Parenthetical overload | dashes, brackets, and clauses contain the material point | promote the content to a main sentence or delete it |
| Pronoun trap | two names or nouns could be `he`, `she`, `it`, `they`, `this`, or `which` | repeat the exact noun or name |
| Modifier drift | phrase appears to describe the wrong actor/action | place it immediately beside its referent |
| List without a spine | many items arrive before the reader knows why | state the governing claim; parallelise and punctuate items |
| Acronym wall | terms arrive before they are defined or differentiated | write the ordinary name, then the abbreviation once |

## Keep a long sentence when

- it follows a main clause with a clear, staged accumulation;
- its punctuation marks a sequence the reader can navigate;
- its length creates necessary suspense, rhythm, comparison, or scope;
- splitting would sever a cause from result or flatten a carefully balanced thought.

## Split a sentence when

- the actor changes;
- a qualification needs independent scrutiny;
- a new claim depends on evidence not yet introduced;
- the reader has to carry unresolved syntax until the end;
- the sentence contains more than one conclusion.

## Micro-tests

Read aloud, then answer: Where did I expect the verb? Which noun does `this` mean? Can I name the actor? What is true even if the qualification is removed? If the answer is unclear, revise the structure before touching the adjectives.
