# Editing Rulebook

Apply the rules in this order. Later passes cannot repair a false or disordered draft.

## 1. Preserve the claim exactly

- Keep facts, figures, chronology, scope, uncertainty, condition, and attribution.
- Preserve the distinction between a false statement and a knowingly false statement; between a policy, proposal, intention, and completed act; and between an ordinary word and a contract-defined one.
- Do not gain vividness by alleging motive, blame, harm, or causation the source does not establish.

## 2. Find the governing sentence

- State the point in a plain test sentence before editing its surroundings.
- Separate event, diagnosis, remedy, evidence, and consequence when a single sentence asks the reader to carry all five.
- Lead with the most compelling verified fact in news and explanation. Do not mechanically cram every detail into the first line.

## 3. Name agency

- Prefer `The audit team missed the warning` to `The warning was missed` when responsibility matters.
- Reanimate a process noun: `The team assessed the case`, not `An assessment of the case was undertaken`.
- Use active voice as the default. Keep the passive only when an actor is unknown, the receiver is the news, or tact, law, or rhythm gives the passive a clear job.

## 4. Keep grammar connected

- Put the main subject and verb near enough to be held together.
- Keep modifiers beside what they modify.
- Repeat a proper name when a pronoun could point to two people, things, or actions.
- Announce lists and give their items parallel grammar. Use repetition when it makes a sequence or contrast easier to follow.
- Replace a long predatory opening with a short orienting phrase or a following sentence.

## 5. Make it concrete

- Trade generic containers - `issue`, `situation`, `conditions`, `activities`, `facilities`, `aspect`, `matter` - for the actual action, object, person, place, or consequence.
- Supply a figure when size, time, cost, risk, or comparison is material. Do not use a number to create false precision.
- In narrative, choose details that reveal character, setting, tension, or change. A catalogue of detail is not a scene.

## 6. Hunt the zombies

Zombie nouns consume verbs and invite passive, abstract prose. Question `implementation`, `assessment`, `authorisation`, `documentation`, `participation`, `utilisation`, and kin. Convert them to a doer and a verb unless the abstraction compresses a necessary complex concept.

## 7. Remove flesh-eaters and pleonasms

- Cut pumped-up phrases: `in relation to`, `in connection with`, `at this point in time`, `for the purpose of`, `in the event that`, `a number of`.
- Test every `there is`, `it is`, `was undertaken`, `has the capacity to`, and weak phrasal construction for a simpler live verb.
- Remove unconscious duplicated sense. Keep deliberate repetition only when it gives emphasis, cadence, irony, politeness, or a logical thread.

## 8. Ration modifiers and imagery

- Ask what every adjective or adverb distinguishes. Let an exact verb do the work first.
- Do not amplify absolutes without a literal reason.
- Replace a dead cliche with a plain fact, fresh image, or deliberate shared shorthand. Do not decorate merely to sound literary.

## 9. State the logic positively

- Say what is true instead of nesting negatives, unless the negation itself matters.
- Avoid contrastive-negation frames such as `not X, but Y` and `rather than X, Y`. State `Y` directly, then add `X` only when the distinction carries material meaning.
- Make `and`, `but`, `yet`, `because`, `if`, and `therefore` carry a real relation.
- In argument, make the hinge explicit: concession, contrast, condition, distinction, or consequence.

## 10. Shape the sound

- Mix loose/cumulative, periodic, balanced, and short landing sentences.
- Read aloud for breath, stress, clarity, unwanted comedy, sameness, and ending force.
- Keep a sentence long when its staged movement, rhythm, or accumulated detail justifies it. Cut it when it makes the reader hold unresolved cargo.
- Use full stops and commas as the normal punctuation. Reserve a colon for a genuine introduction or explanation; reserve an em dash for a purposeful interruption, turn, or apposition. Do not stack either mark for visual drama.

## 10a. Keep the house style consistent

- Use Australian English unless the source, audience, quotation, code, proper name, or required term demands another convention.
- Retain source spelling inside quotations and established product or legal names.

## 11. Choose the right form

- Use a direct news/explanatory lead when the reader needs the result now.
- Use narrative when sequence, character, scene, and delayed knowledge create understanding that a summary cannot.
- Use a taster in long work: a brief consequential glimpse, then a return to the main chronology.
- Use direct address or a question only when it helps the reader inhabit evidence rather than substitutes for it.

## 12. Finish with consequence

- In consequential prose, say who owns the next action, what happens, and what the affected reader can do.
- In narrative, return to or enlarge the central tension with an observed detail.
- In argument, end on the judgment that evidence has earned, not a new unsupported flourish.
- Before finalising, compare the last two sentences with the paragraph's fact map. Delete or recast a sentence that merely repeats an already delivered event, number, or outcome to fill space.
